Who We Are

Our Mission & Vision

Pulled from the "Mission & Vision" Google Doc — edits there appear here automatically.

"Encore Performing Arts inspires and empowers young artists to discover and develop important life skills through unparalleled arts education, mentorship, and extraordinary theater experiences."

Encore is an arts education organization first — a theater company second. We are not a school, not a touring company, and not a community theater in the traditional sense. We are an educational performing arts nonprofit that uses the vehicle of live theater to develop life skills in young artists ages 8 to 18.

Through the lens of theater, we're developing

  • Collaboration and teamwork
  • Critical thinking and complex problem-solving
  • Giving and receiving constructive criticism
  • Communication
  • Confidence
  • Leadership
  • Empathy

The Three Daily Action Principles

Every choice you make as a director should ladder back to one of these three. They're how The Encore Way shows up in the rehearsal room.

01

Nurture Growth Mindsets

Celebrate effort and improvement. Turn mistakes into teachable moments. Treat inexperience as opportunity.

02

Foster a Positive, Professional Environment

Model industry standards. Prioritize safety and emotional well-being over showmanship. Keep drama on stage.

03

Empower Through Personalized Mentorship

Tailor to each young artist. Balance performance skills and emotional intelligence. Foster teamwork.

Non-Negotiables

The Ten Commandments

Pulled from the "Ten Commandments" Google Doc.

  1. Respect is Our Spotlight.No yelling, snapping, or condescending tones. Build up, never break down.
  2. They're Not Just Kids — They're Artists.Always refer to youth as "young artists." Never "kids."
  3. Emotional Intelligence Takes Center Stage.Our primary mission is nurturing EQ. The professional theater experience is the cherry on top.
  4. See Them, Hear Them — On and Off Stage.Proper lighting, working mics, and genuine attentiveness to their concerns.
  5. Safety First, Sequins Second.No stunt or choreography is worth risking a young artist's well-being.
  6. Communication is Key — Set Clear Expectations.Communicate expectations clearly and upfront. Use production meetings (The Purple Box Rule).
  7. Respect the Expertise Ensemble.Stay in your lane. Let each expert lead their domain. We're an ensemble.
  8. Keep the Atmosphere Positive.Maintain an upbeat environment around young artists, parents, and patrons.
  9. Inexperience is Not a Weakness — It's an Opportunity.Treat inexperience as a chance for mentorship, not a flaw.
  10. Keep Perspective — Leave the Drama on Stage.Approach challenges with a calm, solution-oriented mindset.
Your Role

What it means to direct at Encore

Pulled from the "Director Job Description" Google Doc — once we wire up live syncing, edits there will appear here automatically.

A theater director is responsible for overseeing all aspects of a theatrical production — conceptualization, casting, blocking, and overall artistic vision. You'll work closely with the Production Manager, Stage Manager, Music Supervisor, Choreographer, and design team to ensure a cohesive production. Your primary responsibility is to interpret the script and bring it to life on stage, guiding actors, designers, and the production team through rehearsal and performance — and to collaborate with the creative team to establish the visual and emotional tone of the production.

The Mike & Sully Principle

Every Encore production needs role clarity between two functions: technical execution (the Mike / Stage Manager role) and people-centered nurturing (the Sully / Company Manager role). Before rehearsals begin, work with Kristen Stocks to confirm who's holding each role for The Lion King. As the director, you'll lean toward Sully — but expect to flex.

Key Responsibilities

  • Artistic Vision. Conceive and design the overall artistic and visual elements that align with your vision. Create an atmosphere that evokes the time period, setting, and emotional tone of the show.
  • Collaboration. Work closely with actors, musicians, designers, and the production team so your concepts integrate seamlessly with the overarching artistic vision.
  • Blocking and Staging. Design and oversee actor movement on stage. Create dynamic stage pictures and ensure the flow of the production is smooth.
  • Rehearsal Management. Lead rehearsals with clear direction, feedback, and motivation. Work with cast on character development, timing, and delivery.
  • Casting. Assist with casting decisions in collaboration with the music director and choreographer (Respect the Expertise Ensemble).
  • Technical Coordination. Work closely with lighting, set, and costume designers to ensure all elements support the artistic vision and work together harmoniously.
  • Budget Management. Honor and help manage the production's budget — cost estimates, procurement of materials, and cost-effective solutions.

Qualities of a Director

Creativity

A creative and imaginative mindset to develop the visual and emotional aspects of the production that support the script's vision.

Attention to Detail

Meticulous attention to blocking, character development, and overall design — every aspect must be solid and reliable.

Collaborative Skills

Work harmoniously with actors, designers, and fellow team members to convey your vision effectively.

Communication

Articulate your vision clearly to the cast and crew so everyone is on the same page.

Leadership

Guide the production team, make decisive choices, and keep the production on track.

Time Management

Meet deadlines related to rehearsals and performances to ensure the production runs smoothly.

The Production Journey

The director's work moves through nine distinct phases. Open any phase to see what you're responsible for during it.

01 Pre-Production View

Pre-production is, in some ways, the most important part of our production process. With a clear plan and a path to a fully realized show, we avoid many pitfalls along the way. This phase shapes the creative vision, lays logistical plans, and meticulously prepares the artistic and technical elements — generating effective budgeting, scheduling, casting, and design that aligns with both your vision and Encore's vision for the show.

Initial Preparation
  • Obtain a script as soon as one is available
  • Write a mission and vision statement for the production to share with the team
  • Develop a thorough knowledge of the script to support the audition process and casting
  • Participate in all production meetings
Creative Collaboration
  • Communicate with designers, choreographer, and music director about cuts, additions, or adjustments
  • Identify areas in the script needing specific or complicated staging or technical elements
  • Work with the Stage Manager and Production Manager to create the rehearsal schedule and a plan to complete all staging, vocal rehearsals, and choreography by Designer Run
02 Casting View

Discuss ideal cast size, whether young artists will be cut, double-casting needs, and any roles that may require creative solutions.

Scheduling

Audition dates should be scheduled in collaboration with the Producers, Production Manager, Stage Manager, Director, Choreographer, and Music Director. Auditions should ideally be at least 3 weeks before the first rehearsal, with callbacks about 2 weeks before — leaving time to plan around conflicts and recast if needed.

Audition Process
  • Find appropriate sides for callbacks
  • Run auditions and guide the team to find and build an appropriate cast for the show
03 Rehearsals View

Maintaining a well-structured, healthy, inquisitive atmosphere throughout the rehearsal process is essential for nurturing creativity and reaching the production's full potential. Such an environment encourages open dialogue, the exploration of ideas, and the development of Encore's young artists. It fosters trust and collaboration, allowing artists to take risks, make discoveries, and adapt their performances. Inquisitiveness sparks innovation and fresh perspectives — leading to a richer, more dynamic production that resonates with audiences.

Daily Responsibilities
  • Engage in and respect a harmonious, collaborative working environment
  • Be prepared and follow the rehearsal schedule for scenes and blocking
  • Remember you're working with performers who need positive feedback, reminders, and encouragement
  • Educate young artists on performance techniques and lead supportive warm-ups
  • Correct mistakes as needed; have high expectations for execution and overall production vision
Collaboration and Communication
  • Continually communicate with the music director and choreographer to align all production expectations
  • Attend production meetings and update design and production teams on progress, changes, and concerns
04 Designer Run View

All designers come in with a fully realized design, ready to be flexible and collaborate with the rest of the design team. Here we begin to examine all designs and see how they move into one big design that tells one big, clear, moving story.

Depending on your discretion, this is often an opportunity to sit with the lighting designer (LD) and Stage Manager to share specifics about lights — colors, mood, areas that should or shouldn't be lit. The LD takes notes to begin programming the board, and gets lead time to discuss what's possible given the space and lighting instruments available.

  • Attend the designer run with a complete understanding of the staging and readiness to answer designer questions after the run
  • Use this opportunity to catch and work out any issues before moving into the performance space
  • Participate in the production meeting following the designer run to address questions and discrepancies

Important: The designer run is always followed by a production meeting where questions and discrepancies can be addressed. This meeting essentially sets the table for tech.

05 Paper Tech View

The production team — director, stage manager, lighting designer, sound designer, set designer, and other technical crew — go through the script and discuss/plan the technical elements: lighting changes, sound effects, set movements, and other cues.

It's called a "paper tech" because traditionally this run-through goes through the script focusing on the technical details, often using paper and pencil to mark cues, timings, and other technical notes. While actors aren't present, their blocking and movements are discussed and accounted for.

The Show Bible, created by the Stage Manager, is the source of truth for this meeting. It is paramount that at least the first draft of the Bible is ready for this meeting. The goal: ensure all technical elements are coordinated and well-planned before moving into the space.

06 Dry Tech View

Dry tech runs through cues with the entire design team — focusing mainly on set moves and how lights, sounds, and sets coordinate together before adding actors and costumes. A great way to work out bugs before the young artists are in the room.

  • Attend the dry tech meeting with the entire design team to run through cues, focusing on set moves, lights, sounds, and sets coordination
  • Provide input on how elements can be manipulated to facilitate transitions
07 Tech View

Guide all the elements coming together and effectively prepare to hand the show over to the Stage Manager. Ensure all parts are coming together harmoniously and serving the overall vision and storytelling. Continue to coach and note the cast through the changes and challenges that reveal themselves during tech.

Technical Integration
  • Work with the Sound Designer to ensure music, microphones, and other sound elements create a balanced sound
  • Work with the Light Designer to ensure lighting serves the overall vision and storytelling
  • During technical rehearsals, ensure all design elements are maintained and continue to grow
Leadership and Communication
  • Attend production meetings at the end of tech, at the stage manager's discretion
  • Continue collaboration with the production manager, stage manager, and the rest of the design and production team
  • Continue to direct, note, and encourage the cast
08 During the Run View

Once the show is open, your job is essentially done. Continue to attend performances, cheer on the cast, and note the show as you see appropriate.

09 End of the Run View
  • Assist and oversee the return of any borrowed equipment or materials that supported the production
  • Reflect on the production's success and document notes for future reference and improvement

Ongoing Knowledge and Oversight

  • Maintain a thorough understanding of the script and production techniques needed for the current production
  • Communicate regularly with the production manager, stage manager, designers, and other production positions
  • Stay updated on current trends in theater production and performance

Before working with any young artist, every Encore team member completes our child safety training. Commandment #5 is non-negotiable: Safety First, Sequins Second.
